關鍵臨床試驗

以下為此主題的核心隨機試驗與樞紐研究，資料源自 Estes 2025 Key Studies 資料表，已整合於本章。

Core randomized trials and pivotal studies for this topic, from the Estes 2025 Key Studies tables, are integrated into this chapter.

HGUCG

Bladder · Bladder preservation

Kougioumtzopoulou et al, ESTRO, 2025 · N=77

Population: cT2-T4N0M0 muscle-invasive bladder cancer s/p maximal TURBT

Methods: Phase II randomized ➜ bladder preservation RT with concurrent cisplatin vs. ➜ above with nivolumab starting 2 weeks prior to radiation and continuing concurrent and 6 months after Radiation was 60 Gy to whole bladder plus 10 Gy boost

Results: 2-yr LRF 72% vs. 59%, p=0.22 2-yr BCFFS 43% vs. 70% 2-yr OS 61% vs. 87%

Interpretation: OS and CSS was improved with nivolumab added to bladder preservation therapy. LRC did trended to improvement. Phase III trials are warranted.

Mount Sinai Hospital, University of Toronto

Bladder · Bladder preservation

Zlotta et al, Lancet Oncol, 2023 · N=722

Population: cT2-T4N0M0 muscle-invasive bladder cancer, solitary tumors, no hydronephrosis, no extensive or multifocal carcinoma in situ

Methods: Retrospective meta-analysis from three centers, using propensity score matching and inverse probability treatment weighting radical cystectomy compared to bladder preservation

Results: radical cystectomy vs. bladder preservation 5-yr OS 72% vs. 77% 5-yr MFS 74% vs. 75% 5-yr CSS 81% vs. 84% 5-yr DFS 73% vs. 74% (propensity score matching values only are shown here)

Interpretation: This meta-analysis shows favorable outcomes for both radical cystectomy and bladder preservation. OS seems to favor bladder preservation. The study is limited by its retrospective nature. Randomized trials are warranted.

RTOG

Bladder · Bladder preservation

Efstathiou et al, JCO, 2009 Mak et al, JCO, 2014 · N=285, 468

Population: muscle invasive bladder cancer in six RTOG studies (8802, 8903, 9506, 9706, 9906, 0233), T2-T4a

Methods: Adverse effects outcomes: Meta-analysis of 4 RTOG trials. Median f/u 5.4 years. Control outcomes: Meta-analysis of 6 RTOG bladder preservation trials

Results: CR 69% 5-yr intact bladder: 80% 5-yr OS 57%, 10-yr OS 36% 5-yr DSS 71%, 10-yr DSS 65% 5-yr bladder intact DFS 56%, 10-yr 55% 5-yr muscle invasive LF 13%, 10-yr 14% 5-yr non-muscle inv LF 31%, 10-yr 36% 5-yr node recurrence 13%, 10-yr 16% 5-yr DM 31%, 10-yr DM 35% CR rates: 74% in T2, 67% in T3-T4a, 73% in complete TURBT, 56% in incomplete TURBT. No difference in response by histology, hydronephrosis, or grade. Late grade 3 effects: 6% GU, 2% GI No grade 4 or grade 5 toxicity

Interpretation: Bladder preservation leads to favorable outcomes in long-term rates of intact bladder. There is low late adverse effects. Randomized trials are warranted to compare the approach to cystectomy.

Massachusetts General Hospital

Bladder · Bladder preservation

Shipley et al, Urology, 2002 · N=190

Population: cT2-T4a bladder cancer

Methods: Pooled analysis of 5 prospective protocols TURBT then chemoRT to 64-65 Gy if CR after 40 Gy, or those medically unfit for cystectomy.

Results: 5-yr OS 54%, 10-yr OS 36% 5-yr DSS 63%, 10-yr DSS 59% 5-yr DSS with intact bladder 46%, 10-yr 45% Pelvic recurrence 8.4% 1/3 of required salvage cystectomy.

Interpretation: Bladder preservation results in favorable outcomes.

RTOG 8903

Bladder · Neoadjuvant MCV in bladder preservation

Shipley et al, JCO, 1998 · N=123

Population: T2-T4a

Methods: ➜ MCV x2 then 39.6 Gy pelvic RT + concurrent cisplatin vs. ➜ without induction MCV If CR, boost to 64.8Gy and additional dose cisplatin, otherwise cystectomy

Results: 5-yr OS 49% in both arms cCR unchanged, 61% and 55% 5-yr OS with functioning bladder 38% (78% surviving with bladder, 38%/49%) 5-yr DM 33-39% No statistically significant changes

Interpretation: Neoadjuvant MCV prior to bladder preservation did not improve outcomes.

BC2001

Bladder · RT volume, concurrent chemo, hypofx

James et al, NEJM, 2012 Huddart et al, IJROBP, 2014 Hall et al, Eur Urol, 2022 · N=219, 360

Population: T2-T4a

Methods: ➜ standard volume RT vs. ➜ reduced high dose volume RT ➜ concurrent 5FU/MMC vs. ➜ no chemo RT primary endpoint: 2-yr LRC of RHDVRT at most 10% inferior RT dose: Choice of 55 Gy/20 fx or 64 Gy/32 fx. RHDVRT received the rx dose to the GTV and 80% dose to the whole bladder. Standard volume RT received the dose to the whole bladder.

Results: 📌 RT randomization Noninferiority of LRC of standard RT vs. RHDVRT could not be confirmed 2-yr LRC 62% vs. 67% (not noninferior) 5-yr LRC ~55%, 10-yr ~48% inv LRC 2-yr ~81%, 5-yr 76%, 10-yr 69% 2-yr bladder preservation ~89% Grade 3-4 adverse effects 13% in both RT arms 📌 Chemo randomization Chemo improved LRC 5-yr LRC 63% vs. 49% 5-yr inv LRC 79% vs. 64% 5-yr bladder preservation 86% vs. 78% At long term f/u at 9.9 yrs, there were some trends to improvements in DFS, MFS, OS, and CSS with chemo, but no differences, and all CIs crossed 1

Interpretation: Reduced volume high dose RT (RHDVRT) was not noninferior for LC and did not reduce late effects. However overall outcomes seemed favorable. Chemotherapy improves LC and bladder preservation rates. There is a trend to improved OS, CSS, DFS, and MFS when adding 5FU and MMC to radiation; however this is not significant long-term.

BC2001 & BCON combined analysis

Bladder · Hypofractionation

Choudhury et al, Lancet Oncol, 2021 · N=782

Population: Muscle invasive bladder cancer

Methods: Choice of 55 Gy/20 fx or 64 Gy/32 fx in BC2001 and BCON Combined analysis of LC, OS, and adverse effects in these trials to assess noninferiority

Results: 📌 RT randomization Noninferiority of LRC of standard RT vs. RHDVRT could not be confirmed 2-yr LRC 62% vs. 67% (not noninferior) 5-yr LRC ~55%, 10-yr ~48% inv LRC 2-yr ~81%, 5-yr 76%, 10-yr 69% 2-yr bladder preservation ~89% Grade 3-4 effects 13% in both RT arms 📌 Chemo randomization Chemo improved LRC 5-yr LRC 63% vs. 49% 5-yr inv LRC 79% vs. 64% 5-yr bladder preservation 86% vs. 78% At long term f/u at 9.9 yrs, there were some trends to improvements in DFS, MFS, OS, and CSS with chemo, but no differences, and all CIs crossed 1

Interpretation: In this propensity matched analysis of two trials that allowed hypofractionation as physicians' choice, 55 Gy/20 fx is noninferior to 64 Gy/32 fx in LC, OS, and adverse effects. LC with 55 Gy is superior. Randomized trials are warranted.

BART

Bladder · Adjuvant RT

Murthy et al, IJROBP, 2024 Murthy et al, JCO, 2026 · N=153

Population: urothelial bladder cancer s/p radical cystectomy with ≥1 risk factor: pT3-4, pN1-3, nodal yield <10, positive margin, or neoadjuvant chemo for ≥cT3

Methods: Phase III ➜ 🏆 adjuvant 50.4 Gy/28 fx to nodes and SIB 45 Gy to cystectomy bed vs. ➜ obs

Results: 2-yr LRFS 87% vs. 76%, p=0.04 2-yr LRF 8% vs. 27% 2-yr DM 31%, not different 2-yr DFS 72% vs. 59% 2-yr CSS 80% vs. 65% 2-yr OS 70% vs. 57% On subanalysis, benefit with RT in T3-4 and N+ No isolated LF with RT Acute grade 3 effects 1.6% vs. 4.2% Acute grade 2 effects 17.5% vs. 1.1% Late grade 3-4 effects 8.4% vs. 10.5% Late grade ≥2 effects 23.3% vs. 17.5%

Interpretation: Adjuvant radiation therapy in bladder cancer improves LRFS.

NCI, Cairo Univeristy, Egypt

Bladder · Adjuvant RT

Zaghloul et al, JAMA Surg, 2018 Zaghloul et al, ASTRO, 2019 · N=120

Population: ≥pT3b, N+, or grade 3 with negative margins, age ≤70

Methods: Phase II randomized ➜ RT to 45 Gy in 150 cGy BID over 3 wks with 2 cycles gem/cis before and after vs. ➜ RT then adjuvant chemo vs. ➜ gem/cis x4 alone

Results: 2-yr LRFS (chemoRT vs. chemo) 96% vs. 69% 2-yr DFS 68% vs. 56%, p=0.07 2-yr OS 71% vs. 60%, p=0.11 SCC subset 2-yr OS (chemoRT vs. chemo) 71% vs. 43% characteristics: 53% urothelial and 47% SCC

Interpretation: Adjuvant chemotherapy plus RT resulted in improved LRF and trend to improvement in DFS and OS.

GETUG-AFU 30 (protocol)

Bladder · Adjuvant RT

UNICANER, ongoing protocol · N=109

Population: s/p radical cystectomy, pT3a-T4b, any N1-2, or any R1. MIBC pure urothelial carcinoma or mixed (>50% urothelial)

Methods: Phase III ➜ adjuvant 50.4 Gy WPRT vs. ➜ obs

Results: ongoing trial Primary endpoint: pelvic RFS

UTSW

Bladder · Bladder preservation vs. cystectomy

Vashistha et al, IJROBP, 2017 · N=9554

Population: Patients undergoing radical cystectomy or CRT

Methods: Databases searched for randomized, prospective, or restrospective trials evaluating radical cystectomy vs. CRT

Results: 8 studies were eligible for meta-analysis No difference in 5-yr OS, 10-yr OS, 5-yr DSS, 10-yr DSS, or 10-yr PFS Cystectomy arms had higher rates of early complications and minor complications were similar between arms

Interpretation: This meta-analysis showed no difference in OS, DSS, or PFS between cystectomy and bladder preservation. Randomized trials are warranted.

RTOG 0233

Bladder · taxol vs. 5FU

Mitin et al, Lancet Oncol, 2013 · N=97

Population: T2-T4a

Methods: Phase II ➜ TURBT → chemoRT (taxol/cis vs. 5FU/cis) → check response → more chemoRT if T0, Tcis, or Ta → adjuvant gemzar, taxol, cisplatin RT 40.3 Gy BID plus 24 Gy BID boost if downstaged

Results: TC vs. FC: after induction, T0, Ta, Tis: 87% vs. 79% CR 72% vs. 62% 4-yr alive with intact bladder 73% vs. 69% late grade 3-4 RT effects: 11% vs. 6% late grade 3-4 non-RT effects: 24% vs. 34% 67% completed entire taxol and 53% completed entire 5FU chemo

Interpretation: Taxol and cisplatin has favorable control and less adverse effects compared to 5FU/cis in this phase II trial.

Princess Margaret Cancer

Renal · SBRT

Huang et al, Lancet Oncol, 2025 · N=8910

Population: Localized RCC

Methods: Meta-analysis of observational and randomized trials evaluating ➜ SBRT vs. ➜ RFA vs. ➜ microwave ablation vs. ➜ cryoablation

Results: SBRT vs. RFA vs. microwave ablation vs. cryo 5-yr LC 95% vs. 92% vs. 86% vs. 90% grade 3-4 adverse effects 2% vs. 2% vs. 1% vs. 3%

Interpretation: In this meta-analysis SBRT seems to have better local control compared to RFA, microwave ablation, and cryotherapy. All seem to be effective treatment approaches.

Case Western Meta-analysis

Renal · SBRT

Rajoulh et al, ASTRO, 2024 · N=7810

Population: T1-3N0M0 renal cell carcinoma

Methods: Meta-analysis of eGFR in studies evaluating radical nephrectomy, partial nephrectomy, SBRT, ablative therapy

Results: Pre vs post eGFR mean 21.8 RN vs. 5.5 PN vs. 8.6 SBRT vs. 5.1 ablatives (worse with radical nephrectomy, not different with others) Grade 3-5 events 4.7 PN vs. 0.2 SBRT vs. 4.3 ablatives

Interpretation: SBRT, partial nephrectomy, and ablative therapies have the least reduction in eGFR. SBRT has the fewest grade 3-5 events.

FASTRACK II

Renal · SBRT

Siva et al, Lancet Oncol, 2024 · N=70

Population: Medically inoperable RCC with single lesion, biopsy confirmed Median tumor size was 4.6 cm

Methods: Phase II SBRT 26 Gy single fraction for size ≤4 cm SBRT 42 Gy/3 fx for size >4 cm (67% had three fractions)

Results: 1-yr LC 100% CSS 100% 3-yr FFDF 97% 3-yr OS 82% 1-yr eGFR reduction -10.8 mL/min 2-yr eGFR reduction 14.6 mL/min 2-yr split funciton 33% grade 3 adverse effects 10% (none related to renal function) no grade 4-5 adverse effects

Interpretation: SBRT is an effective treatment for primary renal cell carcinoma and with low adverse effects.

RADSTER

Renal · SBRT

Swaminath et al, ASTRO, 2023 · N=24

Population: renal masses ≤4 cm who were inoperable or who declined surgery or active surveillance

Methods: ➜ RFA vs. ➜ SBRT 1-yr biopsy required (see commentary)

Results: 14 had SBRT and 7 had RFA. Crossover occured mostly due to technical inability to perform RFA 1-yr RECIST LC 100% vs. 100% No deaths or DM 1-yr arterial enhancement 23% vs. 83% Mean GFR change ~4 mL/min both groups 1-yr biopsy no residual RCC 100% vs. 31% (plus 15% with minimal residual disease and 54% with evidence of RCC)

Interpretation: SBRT and RFA for small renal tumors both lead to excellent outcomes in this small study. RFA had less post-biopsy residual disease, but LC was still 100% in both arms.

IROCK

Renal · SBRT

Siva et al, Future Oncol, 2016 Siva et al, Cancer, 2018 Correa et al, J Urol, 2019 Siva et al, Lancet Oncol, 2022 · N=various

Population: SBRT for renal tumors, primary or metastatic

Methods: pooled multicenter cohort analysis 30% had solitary kidney

Results: 5-yr LC 96% 2/5-yr CSS 96/92% 2/5-yr PFS 72/64% Single fx SBRT had better LC than fractionated 5-yr median GFR decrease of 14.2 mL/min 4% required dialysis n=2 of grade 4 (duodenal ulcer & gastritis) no grade 3 adverse effects Grade 1-2 adverse effects 37%

Interpretation: SBRT for kidney cancer leads to favorable outcomes and low adverse effects.

London Regional Cancer Program, London, Ontario, Canada

Renal · SBRT meta- analysis

Correa et al, Eur Urol Focus, 2019 · N=372

Population: SBRT for primary renal tumors

Methods: Meta-analysis 26 studies (11 prospective)

Results: LC 97% grade 3-4 adverse effects 1.5% eGFR change -7.7 -Six with pre-exisiting dysfunction needed dialysis -Most common doses: 26 Gy/ 1 fx and 40 Gy/ 5 fx -RCC confirmed in ~80% prior to SBRT

Interpretation: SBRT for primary RCC has excellent safety and LC.

MDACC

Renal · Oligometastasis

Tang et al, Lancet Oncol, 2021 · N=30

Population: ≤5 oligometastases, ≤1 prior systemic therapy, any histology

Methods: Phase II: SBRT to all lesions IMRT 60-70 Gy/10 fx or 52.5-67.5 Gy/10 fx allowed if SBRT constraints not met

Results: Median PFS 22.7 mos 1-yr PFS 64%

Interpretation: SBRT for oligometastatic disease in RCC may allow for deferral of systemic therapy.

Third Spanish Germ Cell Cancer Group

Seminoma · Surveillance vs. chemo

Aparicio et al, JCO, 2011 · N=227

Population: Stage I

Methods: Prospective: survellance if 0-1 risk factors. Carboplatin x2 cycles if 2 risk factors

Results: 10% relapses in surveillance group and 1% relapse in chemo group 3-yr DFS 88% with surveillance, 98% carboplatin 3-yr OS 100% All 14 failures were successfully salvaged.

Interpretation: Surveillance and chemotherapy are both appropriate options for seminoma Stage I.

Rigshospitalet, Copenhagen, Denmark

Seminoma · Surveillance

Daugaard et al, JCO, 2007 · N=695

Population: Stage I seminoma and NSGCT

Methods: Retrospective

Results: After surveillance in stage I, CSS (salvage) still 100%. 17% of seminomas relapsed, most in first year

Interpretation: Observation in stage I seminoma results in low relapses.

MRC TE19/ EORTC 30982

Seminoma · RT vs. chemo

Oliver et al, Lancet, 2005 Olilver et al, JCO, 2011 · N=1447

Population: 70 hospitals; clinical stage I

Methods: ➜ 20-30 Gy to perioartics vs. ➜ carbo AUC 7 x 1 (5:3 ratio) If prior surgery, inguinals were added to the radiation field ("dogleg" field)

Results: 5-yr RFS rates ~95%, not different Higher PA node relapse with carbo 74% vs. 9% But other relapses more common after RT Fewer secondary germ cell tumors after carbo. Contralateral recurrence n=15 vs. n=2 for chemo

Interpretation: Carboplatin is non-inferior to RT.

MRC

Seminoma · RT dose

Jones et al, JCO, 2005 · N=625

Population: Stage I seminoma

Methods: ➜ 30 Gy vs. ➜ 20 Gy🏆 Field: periaortic (or dogleg if prior inguinal surgery)

Results: 5-yr RFS 96-97%, not different Less lethargy and work impairment with lower dose

Interpretation: There is no benefit to 30 Gy RT over 20 Gy. 20 Gy should be used.

MRC

Seminoma · DL vs. PA field

Fossa et al, JCO, 1999 · N=478

Population: Stage I seminoma

Methods: ➜ periaortic + inguinals field ("dogleg") vs. ➜ periaortic field, 30 Gy/15 fx PA fields T10/11 to L5/S1 and including ipsilateral renal hilum

Results: 3-yr RFS (97% vs. 96%) and OS no difference Four pelvic relapses occurred in PA group vs. none in DL group (SS) Improved rates of diarrhea, leukopenia, nausea, vomiting, azoospermia in PA group.

Interpretation: PA field has decreased morbidity compared to dogleg and should be standard.

NCI

Seminoma · Second malignancy

Travis et al, JNCI, 2005 · N=50576

Population: 1-yr survivors of testicular cancer from American and European registries

Methods: Retrospective review: Analysis of risk of second solid cancers

Results: Risk of 2nd cancer remains elevated till at least 35 years after tx. Risk of solid tumors in lung, pleura, esophagus, gastric, colon, bladder and pancreas. RR: RT 2.0, chemo 1.8, both 2.9

Interpretation: The risk of 2nd cancer in testicular cancer after treatment continues to rise even after 35 years.
