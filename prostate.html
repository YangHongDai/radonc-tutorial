攝護腺癌

局限性、術後、轉移性攝護腺癌與系統性治療。

Localized, post-prostatectomy, metastatic; systemic therapy & RT regimens.

風險分層

NCCN 風險分層

  • Very low / Low: PSA < 10, GS ≤ 6, T1c–T2a
  • Favorable intermediate (FIR): single IR factor + < 50% cores + GG 1–2
  • Unfavorable IR (UIR): 2+ IR factors / GG 3 / > 50% cores
  • High / Very high: GS ≥ 8, PSA > 20, T3a+
  • Very low / Low: PSA < 10, GS ≤ 6, T1c–T2a
  • Favorable intermediate (FIR): single IR factor + < 50% cores + GG 1–2
  • Unfavorable IR (UIR): 2+ IR factors / GG 3 / > 50% cores
  • High / Very high: GS ≥ 8, PSA > 20, T3a+
劑量與分次

劑量與大分次

標準: 78–80 Gy standard fractionation。

Moderate hypofx: CHHiP (60 Gy/20 fx) / PROFIT (60 Gy/20 fx) — 非劣。

Ultra-hypofx / SBRT: HYPO-RT-PC (42.7 Gy/7 fx)、PACE-B (36.25 Gy/5 fx) — 非劣。

ADT + RT (STAMPEDE, Bolla EORTC 22961):
· FIR：短程 ADT 4 個月
· UIR：ADT 4–6 個月
· 高風險：長程 ADT 18–36 個月

Standard: 78–80 Gy standard fractionation.

Moderate hypofractionation: CHHiP (60 Gy/20 fx) / PROFIT (60 Gy/20 fx) — non-inferior.

Ultra-hypofx / SBRT: HYPO-RT-PC (42.7 Gy/7 fx); PACE-B (36.25 Gy/5 fx) — non-inferior.

ADT + RT (STAMPEDE, Bolla EORTC 22961):
· FIR: short-course ADT 4 mo
· UIR: ADT 4–6 mo
· High risk: long-course ADT 18–36 mo

術後 RT

術後放射治療

Adjuvant vs early salvage (RADICALS-RT, GETUG-17, RAVES): 早期 salvage 與 adjuvant 相當，且副作用較少。

RTOG 9601: Salvage RT + bicalutamide 2 yr 相對 RT alone 提升 OS (但主要獲益在 PSA > 0.7)。

SPPORT (NRG 0534): Salvage RT + short-term ADT + pelvic nodal RT 提升 fPFS。

Adjuvant vs early salvage (RADICALS-RT, GETUG-17, RAVES): Early salvage equivalent to adjuvant with less toxicity.

RTOG 9601: Salvage RT + 2-yr bicalutamide improves OS vs RT alone (benefit mainly in PSA > 0.7).

SPPORT (NRG 0534): Salvage RT + short-term ADT + pelvic nodal RT improves fPFS.

ProtecT ⭐

RT vs. surgery vs. observation · Low to int risk

Hamdy et al, NEJM, 2016 Donovan et al, NEJM, 2016 Neal et al, Eur Urol, 2019 Lane et al, BJU Int, 2022 Hamdy et al, NEJM, 2023 Donovan et al, NEJM Evid, 2023 · N=1643

Population: PSA detected prostate cancer (any Gleason score) Age 50-69 (Characteristics: Median age 62 77% Gleason 6, 20% Gleason 7, 2% Gleason 8-10, 76% with T1c)

Methods: ➜ Active surveillance (55% had treatment) vs. ➜ 🏆 Radical prostatectomy vs. ➜ 🏆 RT 74 Gy/ 37 fx (or brachy) + 3-6 mos ADT 3DCRT 74 Gy/37 fx LDR brachytherapy allowed (11%) Most surgeries were nerve-sparing Adjuvant and salvage RT were allowed

Results: -At 15 years, with RT vs. surgery, there is no difference in PCM, DM, disease progression, ADT salvage, or all deaths. -AS had worse DM, disease progression, and ADT compared to RP and RT. However 15-yr PCM and all cause mortality were similar. Surveillance vs. RP vs. RT: 10-yr PCM 1.85% vs. 0.67% vs. 0.73% 15-yr PCM 2.2% vs. 1.5% vs. 2.1% 10-yr DM 5.6% vs. 2.4% vs. 2.7% 15-yr DM 7.1% vs. 3.5% vs. 3.7% 10-yr ADT salvage 8.7% vs. 4.8% vs. 5.6% 15-yr ADT salvage 9.4% vs. 5.3% vs. 5.6% 10-yr clinical progression 20% vs. 5.9% vs. 6.6% 15-yr clinical progression 21% vs. 8.0% vs. 8.4% 10-yr all deat…

Interpretation: RT+ADT and radical prostatectomy show no difference in prostate cancer mortality or DM. Active surveillance had worse DM and ADT salvage, but this did not lead to worse PCM at 15 years. RT+ADT and surgery have differing adverse effect profiles. Surgery has worse incontinence and sexual dysfunction. RT+ADT also leads to some ED at mid term f/u, then ED eventually converges. RT has slightly worse long term loose stool, blood in stool, and GI incontinence compared to AS. Late nocturia is similar between RT and AS. 12-yr adverse effects results were similar.

PACE-A "Battle of the Robots"

RT vs. surgery · Low to int risk

As et al, Eur Urol, 2024 · N=123

Population: low or intermediate risk (92% were intermediate) T1-T2, Gleason ≤3+4=7, PSA ≤20

Methods: ➜ laparoscopic or robotic assisted prostatectomy vs. ➜ SBRT 36.25 Gy to PTV, 40 Gy to CTV (daily or QOD) to prostate ADT, SpaceOAR not allowed Primary endpoints: 2-yr PROs

Results: 2-yr GU using any pads 50% vs. 6.5% 2-yr CTCAE grade ≥2 GU score 7.0% vs. 9.4% 2-yr CTCAE grade 3 GU 2.3% vs. 0 No difference in urinary subdomain score 2-yr CTCAE grade ≥2 GI zero in both arms 2-yr CTCAE grade 1 GI 4.7% vs. 21% 2-yr EPIC bowel domain score 100 vs. 87.5 2-yr EPIC sexual domain score 18 vs. 62.5 2-yr grade ≥2 erectile dysfunction 63% vs. 18% 2-yr grade 3 ED 5.3% vs. 2.0% 2-yr CTCAE grade 4-5 adverse effects zero in all arms

Interpretation: SBRT has less GU incontinence and sexual dysfunction compared to surgery. SBRT had more bowel symptoms. GI adverse effects was very low. Hydrogel spacer was not used in this study.

PROTEUS

Surgery & peri-op ADT · Peri-op ADT

Taplin M et al, NEJM, 2026 · N=2109

Population: high risk prostate cancer defined as: •Gleason 4+3=7 and Gleason 8 in ≥6 cores •Gleason 4+3=7 and Gleason 8 in ≥3 cores and PSA ≥20 •Gleason 8 in ≥2 cores with 80% involvement •Gleason 9-10 in ≥1 core

Methods: ➜ 6 mos apalutamide and ADT → surgery → 6 mos apalutamide and ADT vs. ➜ peri-op 6 mos ADT plus placebo Biochemical failure defined as two consecutive rises to ≥0.2 Imaging q6 mos after BF

Results: pCR or MRD 9% vs. 1% 5-yr MFS 78% vs. 74% 5-yr EFS 49% vs. 40% 4-yr freedom from disease 22% vs 18% Adverse effects during treatment Grade 3-4 adverse effects 40% vs. 31% Urinary incontinence grade 1-2 in about 50% both arms Urinary incontinence grade 3 in 1-2% both arms Median time to testosterone recovery from end of 12 mos ADT 8.1 vs. 6.6 mos Any subsequent therapy 42% vs. 57% Post op radiation 11% vs. 21% (of all patients) (Calculated from Table s4) Post op radiation 27% vs. 37% (of those who had any additional treatment)

Interpretation: Peri-op apalutamide added to ADT improved response rates, MFS, and EFS.

PIVOT

Watchful waiting · RP vs. watchful waiting

Wilt et al, NEJM, 2012 Wilt et al, NEJM, 2017 · N=740

Population: T1-T2Nx, PSA<50, Age<75, negative bone scan, life expectancy >10 years 40% low risk, 33% medium risk, 22% high risk Mean age 67 median PSA 7.8 10 year f/u

Methods: ➜ Watchful waiting vs. ➜ Radical prostatectomy

Results: On 12 yr f/u, radical prostatectomy resulted in reduced all cause mortality for PSA >10. Those with intermediate or high risk were close to a difference (p 0.07). With RP 5.8% died due to cancer or treatment, and with obs 8.4% died, p=0.09. At 19.5 years follow-up: death in 67% obs vs. 61% surgery, p=0.06 death from cancer or treatment 7.4% vs. 11.4%, p=0.06 Surgery may have led to improved mortality in int risk disease (difference 14.5%) Surgery did not improve mortality in low or high risk disease Urinary dysfunction and erectile and sexual function worse with surgery

Interpretation: There is a trend to benefit in OS with surgery over observation, especially in those with intermediate risk disease. Surgery leads to notable adverse effects.

Johns Hopkins

Active Surveillance · Active Surveillance in very low risk

Tosoian et al, JCO, 2011 Tosoian et al, JCO, 2015 · N=1298

Population: Very low risk and low risk: T1c, PSA density <0.15, GS ≤6 with ≤2 cores, <50% of any core

Methods: Phase II active surveillance. Repeat biopsy every year. PSA and DRE q6mos. Treatment if enrollment criteria no longer met

Results: 15-yr OS 69%, 15-yr CSS 99.9%, 15-yr MFS 99.4% Grade reclassification: 10-yr 26%, 15-yr 31% Incidence of curative intervention: 10-yr 50%, 15-yr 57% Median treatment free survival 8.5 yrs

Interpretation: Long term active surveillance in very low and low risk prostate cancer seems acceptable, leading to high survival and low DM.

Sunnybrook, Toronto

Active Surveillance · Active Surveillance

Population: Low or intermediate risk patients. The study later only enrolled low risk patients mean age 67.8 74% T1c 84% Gleason ≤6 13% Gleason 3+4=7 87% PSA <10 11% PSA 10-15 25% intermediate risk

Interpretation: Long term active surveillance in low risk prostate cancer seems acceptable, leading to high survival and low DM.

SPCG-4

Watchful waiting · RP vs. watchful waiting

Bill-Axelson et al, NEJM, 2005 Bill-Axelson et al, NEJM, 2014 Bill-Axelson et al, NEJM, 2018 · N=695

Population: age <75, T1-T2, operable, life expectancy >10yrs, PSA<50, negative bone scan Characteristics: mean age 65 13% gleason 2-4, 48% gleason 5-6, 22% 7, 4% 8-9 Mean PSA 13. 29% PSA 10-20, 20% PSA >20

Methods: ➜ Watchful waiting vs. ➜ Radical prostatectomy ADT started for symptomatic local progression or distant disease

Results: 29-yr f/u -NNT to prevent one death: 8.3 -Mean OS absolute benefit of 2.9 yrs -ECE had 5x risk of death, Gleason 7 had 10x risk death 23-yr Death due to prostate cancer 31% WW vs. 20% RP 23-yr Any mortality 84% vs. 72% -In age <65, 34% vs. 18% deaths from prostate cancer, and less difference if age 65 or older. -Deaths from prostate cancer in intermediate risk group 39% vs. 15%. No difference in low or high risk group OS benefit in low risk age <65 23-yr DM 43% vs. 27%. Benefit in both age groups, low and int risk ADT used in 67% vs. 43%. Benefit in both age groups, and all risk groups

Interpretation: At 29 year follow-up, OS benefit of 2.9 years with RP. ECE and Gleason 7 highly increase risk of death. Reduced mortality with surgery with 23 year follow-up. The benefit is largest in men <65 years of age.

Screening · Meta-analysis

Population: CAP, ERSPC, PLCO, Quebec, Stockholm

Methods: Meta-analysis of five PSA screening trials

Results: PCSM beyond 12 years resulted in a relative 9% reduction in PCSM, p<0.001 PCSM beyond 8 yrs per 10,000 108 vs. 98 patients

Interpretation: PSA screening reduces PCSM.

ERSPC and PLCO reanalysis

Screening · PSA and DRE

Tsodikov et al, Ann Intern Med, 2017 · N=88921 38343

Population: ERSPC: ages 55-69 PLCO: ages 55-74

Methods: Re-analysis of PLCO and ERSPC trials taking in to account those who were actually screened vs. not and lead time bias. Cox regression analysis

Results: Screening estimated to give 7-9% reduction in PCSM per year of mean lead time 11-year follow-up ERSPC: 25-31% relative benefit in PCSM PLCO: 27-32% relative benefit in PCSM

Interpretation: The ERSPC and PLCO screening studies show potential benefit screening for prostate cancer.

Prostate, Lung, Colorectal, Ovarian (PLCO)

Screening · PSA and DRE

Andrioloe et al, NEJM, 2009 Crawford et al, JCO, 2011 · N=76693

Population: Age 55-74

Methods: ➜ PSA screening q6 yrs and DRE q4 yrs vs. ➜ usual care

Results: Screening reduced number of deaths, 22 vs. 38. 10-yr NNT 5 to prevent one death if no comorbidity. If at least 1 comorbidity, no benefit

SELECT

Prevention · Selenium and Vitamin E

Lippman et al, JAMA, 2009 Klein et al, JAMA, 2011 · N=35533

Population: age 55 or older (if black, age ≥ 50), PSA≤4, negative DRE

Methods: ➜ Selenium + placebo vs. ➜ vitamin E + placebo vs. ➜ selenium + vitamin E vs. ➜ two placebos

GETUG 18

Dose escalation · High risk

Hennequin et al, ASCO GU, 2024 · N=505

Population: High risk prostate cancer: PSA ≥20, Gleason score ≥8, or T3-T4

Methods: ➜ 70 Gy/ 35 fx vs. ➜ 🏆 80 Gy/ 40 fx ADT 3 years in all

Results: 5-yr OS 89% vs. 83% 10-yr OS 77% vs. 66% 5-yr CSS 99% vs. 97% 10-yr CSS 96% vs. 90% 5-yr PFS 91% vs. 88% 10-yr PFS 84% vs. 72% Late adverse effects not different

Kerkmeijer et al, JCO 2021 Guricová et al, JCO, 2025 · N=571

Population: Intermediate and high risk (84% high risk)

Methods: ➜ 77 Gy/ 35 fx in 2.2 Gy vs. ➜ 🏆 Above + SIB focal boost up to 95 Gy to MRI nodules as feasible with priority to OARs (95 Gy achieved in 20%)
